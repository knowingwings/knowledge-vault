## Part 4: Process Reflection (15 min)
- **What worked:** The **consultancy-lab → TBRo-lab routine is functional** — the daily 09:30-12:30 anchor plus the lab being a place I'm already in work-mode is doing real work (the environment-as-substrate principle landing in practice). Also a sharp meta-observation: *this week's visible wins (grades) are the lagged output of previous weeks' work* — so "what worked this week" is partly unanswerable in-week; the coursework setup that produced the grades was running weeks ago. Worth holding when judging a week: output lags effort, so a low-feeling week can be banking wins that surface later (and vice versa).
- **What didn't:** **Substrate broke.** Exercise fell off hard (1 run vs the 4-5 target), and the **sleep protocol was entirely broken all week.** These aren't side-notes to the crash — they're prime suspects *in* it. A week of broken sleep + minimal exercise would produce evening crashes on its own, independent of (and compounding) the schedule step-change. So the "adaptation lag" reading is confounded: I can't tell how much of the crash is genuine transition cost vs simple sleep-debt + deconditioning, because all three hit at once. **That's actually good news** — two of the three suspects are directly fixable.
- **Adjustment to try:** **Get substrate back on track as the week's headline — and treat it as the crash experiment.** Specifically: (1) **sleep protocol restored** (phone out of room, hardstop) — the single highest-leverage lever, and the cleanest test: if the crash eases as sleep returns, it was substantially sleep-debt, not immutable schedule-adaptation; (2) **exercise back in**, but designed *around* the crash — runs/calisthenics inside the day or at the labspace, not booked for the dead evenings (per the Part 2 finding that evenings are structurally low-yield while this pattern holds). Substrate is the one lever that's reliably worked all month; pointing it directly at the crash is the move.
