## 1. The One-Line Thesis

**Decentralised multi-robot autonomy for assembly and inspection in extreme, human-inaccessible
environments — proven and monetised subsea today ("Sea"), enabling large-scale orbital and
planetary construction tomorrow ("Space").**

- **Core competency (the *intended* moat — unproven, see §2.5):** decentralised coordination +
  manipulation — robots that divide and execute build/inspect/maintain tasks autonomously,
  without central control or a human in the loop. Roots in the BEng dissertation (decentralised
  task allocation across mobile manipulators — *which did not validate its core claims*) and the
  free-time mechanism work (actuators, reducers). A *direction to prove*, not a defensibility
  you currently hold.
- **Founder identity:** systems architect with a hardware bias — full-stack mechatronics,
  not single-layer specialist. Owns the architecture; goes deep on the hardest current
  sub-problem; uses breadth as *integration leverage*, not as a mandate to do every layer.
- **Tagline (fixed):** *From Sea to Space.* This is a genuine technology-transfer thesis,
  not decoration: the same coordination/manipulation core spans a continuum of hostile,
  latency-bound, inaccessible environments.

## 2. The Disciplines (the through-lines that protect everything else)

These recurred across every part of the plan. They are the guardrails.

1. **Ruthless early focus.** One wedge, world-class. Breadth is the *reward* for focus,
   never the founding plan. (SpaceX nailed one rocket before Starlink/Mars.)
2. **Loose timelines, firm destination.** The 2036 dates are aspirational. Getting the
   foundations right beats hitting a date.
3. **Sequencing over simultaneity.** The failure mode for a visionary founder is doing
   everything at once and exhausting a fragile company. Order: company viability → internal
   capability → external mission.
4. **Control is one problem, not several.** Values (end-use), capital (anti-VC-capture),
   and wealth extraction all reduce to *retaining decision-control*. Solve it once,
   structurally, at incorporation.
5. **Selective vertical integration only.** Integrate the true moat (coordination core, key
   mechanisms). Partner/buy everything else (sonar, launch, raw manufacturing). Total
   vertical integration = the chaebol you explicitly don't want, and is incompatible with
   the focused-CTO life you do want.

## 2.5. Reality Check — Odds, Risks & Kill-Criteria

*The rest of this document is mostly "the coherent plan if it works." This section is the
counterweight: how likely, how it fails, when to change course. Read it first when revisiting.*

**The dominant risk is not the cap table — it's survival.** Deep-tech hardware startups fail
far more often than they succeed, and most of the sophisticated structure below (control
architecture, wealth extraction, give-back) is **moot if the company doesn't reach viability at
all**. The hard part is not dying long enough to matter. Treat §6–§9 as "the good version *if*
we get there," not as the main challenge. The main challenge is §3→§4: get good, pick a real
wedge, build something someone pays for.

**The wedge is a direction to investigate, not an established moat.** The decentralised
multi-robot thesis is (a) *unproven by you* — your own dissertation candidly did not validate
its core theoretical claims — and (b) *genuinely contested* — centralised/hierarchical control
often wins near-term on verifiability, safety-certification, and simplicity, which matter
enormously for irreplaceable assets. Decentralised is *forced* mainly at large scale or high
latency (big structures; Mars). So point your learning and proof-of-capability work at it;
don't claim it as defensibility yet. **Validate before betting the company on it.**

**Load-bearing mechanisms that are contingent:**
- **Dual-class control** may be unavailable in-jurisdiction **[VERIFY]** or rejected by
  investors (first-time founder, hard seed = low leverage). *Fallback:* control leans harder on
  board structure, protective-provision discipline, and investor-type selection (§6). Don't
  assume the share-class lever exists.
- **Non-VC / patient capital** for capital-intensive orbital hardware may not be *enough* — the
  durable-private-company model (§7) could collide with needing large VC to build at all. If so,
  control-via-terms matters even more, and "liquidly comfortable without an exit" may not
  survive the capital need.
- **Guernsey R&D talent attraction** (§5) is unsolved beyond the friend-network.

**Kill-criteria / change-course triggers — decide these *now*, while unemotional:**
- The apprenticeship reveals you'd rather be a *brilliant employee* than a founder. (Valid
  outcome — many of the happiest engineers never found. No shame in it.)
- After honest effort, the wedge proves uneconomic or you can't demonstrate a defensible
  version → pivot the wedge, or reconsider founding.
- You cannot raise the needed capital without ceding the control that makes the plan coherent →
  re-evaluate whether *this* company is worth doing on those terms.
- The subsea "Sea" market proves you can't reach revenue before capital runs out → the
  sea-funds-space bridge fails; rethink the thesis.
- **A pre-set personal line is crossed** — health, relationships, finances, years-spent. Define
  it concretely now (e.g. "if past [age], sunk [£X] and [N] years, with no space-qualified
  hardware or sustainable revenue, I stop and take stock"). The vision doc's non-negotiables
  (relationships, health, no burnout) are the *check* on this brief's intensity — they are
  allowed to win.

**Fallback if founding never happens:** you'd still be a senior space/subsea robotics engineer
with rare full-stack manipulator + autonomy experience — strong, employable, well-paid. The
downside of the founding path is opportunity cost and time, not destitution. Naming that makes
the risk *takeable* rather than terrifying.

> Base-rate humility: hold the whole plan as a *set of bets with odds*, not a roadmap.
> Individually reasonable, collectively long — that's what ambitious founding is. Never let
> confident *prose* masquerade as confident *probability*.

## 6. Control Architecture (the connective tissue)

**The insight:** control and economic ownership are *separable*. You do NOT need to own most
of the shares to keep control. This dissolves the chaebol/family-holdco-conglomerate instinct.

Mechanisms (conceptual — **[VERIFY all with a cross-border corporate lawyer]**; viability and
investor acceptance vary by jurisdiction):

1. **Dual-class / weighted-voting founder shares.** Hold a minority of *economics* but a
   majority of *votes* (e.g. 10:1). Lets you raise + dilute economically while keeping control.
   The single most important tool *if available*. *Must be designed in at incorporation —
   near-impossible to retrofit.* **Contingency (§2.5):** may be unavailable under Guernsey
   company law **[VERIFY]** or rejected by investors given first-time-founder leverage — so
   treat board structure (2) and protective-provision discipline (3) as the *robust core* and
   dual-class as the *bonus lever*, not the foundation.
2. **Board composition.** Who appoints directors and the balance of the board. More important
   day-to-day than share votes. Founder appointment rights / independent-balanced board so no
   investor bloc dominates. This is where the "fund rep forces decisions against founders"
   horror (your friend's-dad story) is prevented.
3. **Protective provisions / reserved matters.** Keep investor-consent items to genuinely
   major events (sale, new shares, big debt) — out of operations. Term-sheet literacy.
4. **Charter / constitutional mission- & end-use locks.** Write end-use restrictions
   (national/allied-only; no regimes you object to) into constitutional docs so they survive.
   **[VERIFY — jurisdiction-dependent]**

**Values ⇄ control are the SAME problem.** Your Stark fear (tech serving regimes you oppose)
is solved by the *same* control machinery as your anti-VC-capture goal: retain end-use /
contract-acceptance veto via founder control. Defence becomes acceptable *iff* you control
who it serves. Caveat: end-use control is never perfect (proliferation, allies' politics shift
over decades) — manageable risk, not zero.

**VC stance:** not blanket avoidance — the enemy is *control-transferring terms*, not capital.
Take capital on terms that don't transfer control (founder-majority board, dual-class,
narrow protective provisions, patient/strategic/government investor types). Understand the
term sheet better than the investor; never trade control for headline valuation.

**Note:** your finance-network (Grant Thornton liquidator friend etc.) sees the *aftermath* of
these structures; *designing* the cap table is specialist startup-corporate-lawyer work — the
likely gap in your free-advice coverage.

## 7. Wealth Extraction — "Liquidly Comfortable, Not Paper Rich"

You identified the core founder wealth trap years early. Most founder wealth is illiquid paper
(equity value, real only at a liquidity event). Your control-retention goals tend to *keep*
wealth in paper form — a genuine tension to design around.

Mechanisms (**[VERIFY tax treatment — highly residency/domicile-dependent]**):
- **Founder salary** — the early baseline for "comfortable." Modest early (capital is precious),
  substantial once funded/revenue-generating. Clean; doesn't touch equity/control.
- **Secondary sales** — sell *some* existing shares (usually to incoming investors in a round)
  for cash, converting paper→liquid *without* selling the company and (with dual-class)
  *without* losing voting control. **The key control-preserving liquidity mechanism for you.**
- **Dividends** — *do* exist for private companies (your earlier doubt: not public-only). But
  need *distributable profits* → realistically late-stage for capital-hungry deep-tech. Once
  profitable & founder-controlled, *you* decide to distribute — the closest thing to your ideal.
- **Liquidity event (sale/IPO)** — the big paper→liquid conversion, but the control-losing
  event you're wary of. You don't have to plan for an exit.

**The model this points to:** a **durable, profitable, founder-controlled private company** you
*keep and run* — wealth via salary + periodic secondaries + eventual dividends — rather than
the VC "starve-the-founder, chase-a-terminal-exit" flip. This aligns with *every* other goal
(control, anti-VC, long-term CTO, Guernsey permanence, give-back).
- Tension: durable-private-hold is structurally incompatible with heavy VC (they need an exit)
  → reinforces the lean toward strategic/government/revenue/patient capital.
- Reality: "comfortable" likely arrives years 5–10, not 1–3 (capital intensity → lean early
  years). Plan personal finances accordingly (cash cushion; don't over-extend early).
- **Guernsey tax position may make these extraction routes far more efficient** (no CGT;
  dividend treatment) than for a UK-resident founder — a place the Guernsey angle pays off in
  substance, not sentiment. **[VERIFY — your GT friend + a tax advisor.]**

## 9. The Give-Back Mission (sequence LAST — it needs a healthy company)

Genuine strategic value, not just sentiment: a mission-rooted founder roots a company deeper
than tax arbitrage does (won't relocate the moment a spreadsheet says to) — part of what makes
Guernsey-siting *defensible*. But it must be *built toward*, not launched alongside incorporation
(a capital-burning early startup can't fund schools programmes without straining itself).

- **FIRST-style schools tournament** (FLL/FTC more realistic than full FRC for island scale):
  binding constraint is usually local engineering mentors — which your company *is*. Flywheel:
  engineers mentor → schools generate future hires → engineering gains visibility vs fintech.
  Likely *part-fundable/partnerable* with Education/Sport/Culture committee + Innovate Guernsey
  (eases cost + deepens govt relationship).
- **Tektronix-style "night schools"** (line tech → engineer ladder): not just philanthropy — a
  *workforce-development strategy* that partly solves your own island hiring problem and
  addresses the "Guerns are brilliant but funnel into fintech" *misallocation*. Incentivise
  teaching via *paid protected hours* (not unpaid evenings) + a recognised senior-progression
  track. Informal internal upskilling early; formal *accredited* courses later via partnership
  (TGI / college / existing further-ed structures — don't own accreditation alone).
- **Sequence:** company viability → internal pipeline (serves the company directly) → external
  schools/courses (ideally co-funded, once you have institutional standing).
