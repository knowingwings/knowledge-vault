# Burnout, motivation, and the LLM-assisted productivity surge

## Premise
Three years of "spinning wheels" on projects: starting things, not finishing them. Then ~April 2026, a sudden shift to actually starting *and* completing things — coinciding with structured LLM use as a thinking partner. Energy crashes still happen (29/04: 2/5 after a string of 4s). The crashes might not be aberrations; they might be the baseline state briefly visible through the new productive rhythm.

## What the essay could explore
- **What changed when LLMs entered the workflow?** Not "they did the work" — they didn't. So what specifically shifted? Hypotheses:
  - Lower activation energy for cold-starting tasks (rubber-ducking removes the blank-page problem)
  - Externalised executive function — the LLM holds the plan so the user doesn't have to keep it loaded
  - Continuous reflection rhythm (daily/weekly/monthly) becomes maintainable when assisted
  - Decomposition that the user couldn't / wouldn't do unassisted (e.g. "ship video" → 12 subticks)
- **Is the productive rhythm fragile?** If yes, what specifically would collapse it? (LLM access removed; reflection cadence broken; fitness staying low; another project failure mode that re-anchors the "wheel-spinning" identity.)
- **Burnout vs depression vs ADHD-adjacent vs values-misalignment** — the underlying state isn't named yet. Each has different implications. Worth being specific rather than catch-all "burnout."
- **Is the productivity itself avoidance?** Working hard on the wrong thing can mask the deeper question: *is what I'm doing benefiting me long-term?* (Carried over from W17 reflection.)
- **What would a "burnout-aware operating system" look like?** Designed to *expect* baseline lows rather than push through them. How do you preserve momentum without paying for it later?
