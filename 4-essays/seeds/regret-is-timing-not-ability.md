# I am already living the dream, and the regret is timing not ability

## Premise
Surfaced 26/06/2026 on a good-energy day. I realised that what I am actually doing is the thing my younger self dreamed of: autonomous vehicles in the mornings (the AB consultancy), an exoskeleton and a rover in the afternoons (the RA piano-exo and Team Bath Roving), and my own robots in the evenings. The 5, 10 and 15-year-old versions of me would be thrilled. I am not failing to apply myself; I am visibly applying myself across exactly the domains I always wanted.

## Tension
If I am clearly living a version of the dream, what is the long-running regret actually about? It is not capability or direction (both are disproven by the present). The real regret is narrower and sharper: I did not start sooner. I told myself I would get going on these threads (the RA especially) both when I started at Bath and again at the start of semester 2, delayed, and the delay had a concrete cost (the RA might have been funded if I had asked when funding was around). It is an initiation problem, not an ability problem.

## Potential thesis
The vague "I am not living up to my potential" regret is usually a mislabelled, more specific, and more fixable thing: a gap between deciding to do something and actually starting it. Naming it precisely (initiation / timing, the silent-plans tax) turns a corrosive identity-level story ("I waste my potential") into a tractable behavioural one ("I delay acting on stated intentions, and windows close, so pre-commit and start"). The cure is not "be more capable" (already true), it is "act on stated intentions promptly."

## What the essay could explore
- Why "not living up to my potential" is almost always a mis-diagnosis, and how to find the specific, fixable thing underneath it.
- Initiation cost: the measurable price of delay (lost funding, lost time, closed windows) versus the imagined price of "wasted potential."
- The complement to joy-stripped-by-structure: there the joy is suppressed; here it is being accessed, and the lesson is about timing rather than structure or direction.
- Pre-commitment as the lever (the run-treatment / silent-plans tax): the same fix as for the can't-start pattern.
- The role of perspective: noticing you are already doing the dream is itself what lets you reframe the regret accurately.
