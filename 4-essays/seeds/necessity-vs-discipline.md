# Necessity > discipline, and the "have but don't use" pattern

## Premise
Recurring observation, sharpened 04/05/2026 when I finally set up input shaper, KAMP, and printer velocity tuning — all things I'd owned for ~2 years and known were available. None of them happened until project speed *required* them (5hr print → 2hr made the iteration loop for Reducer redesign viable).

The lazy-self diagnostic ("why don't I just discipline myself to do this?") is the wrong question. The right one: **why isn't it necessary yet?** When you find the answer, the deferred work either happens automatically or you discover it genuinely shouldn't be a priority.

## The pattern, in inventory form
Things I have / know / could deploy but haven't, across the vault:
- Languages (have flashcard apps, basic skills, motivation)
- Calisthenics (no equipment needed, know the routines)
- LARDER (own app, know it has features I haven't tested)
- Form 1 SLA fix (have parts, knowledge — bounded by location)
- The Arduino-as-ISP reflash (know how, have parts)
- Numerous tools / methods in software development

Each one waits for a forcing function.

## What the essay could explore
- **Discipline as a moralised explanation for a structural problem.** "I should be more disciplined" is what people say when they can't see the structural reason something isn't happening. It's almost always a misdiagnosis.
- **Necessity creation as a skill.** Stake-building, public commitment, sequencing work so the deferred item becomes a blocker for something you actually care about. The healthy version is *deliberate necessity*; the unhealthy version is *running to crisis*.
- **The trade-off between optionality and forcing.** Keeping options open (the "this would be nice to learn") preserves freedom but defers action indefinitely. Closing options ("I have to do X by Friday or Y breaks") creates motion. Both are valid; the question is when each fits.
- **When discipline DOES work.** Probably for short bursts, for things with high felt necessity (deadlines, social commitment), for habits already partially established. Probably NOT for cold-start adoption of "should-have" tools.
- **The depressive/burnt-out variant.** When energy is broken, even high-necessity items stop landing. Necessity is necessary but not sufficient. Pairs with the burnout-baseline + hydration-foundation memories.
